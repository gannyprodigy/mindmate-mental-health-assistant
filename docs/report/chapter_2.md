# Chapter 2: Literature Review and System Study

## 2.1 Introduction

The purpose of this chapter is to situate MindMate within the existing body of scholarly and practical work on student mental health, digital interventions, and the data-science techniques that underpin them. A literature review in an applied data-science project serves two complementary functions. First, it establishes the clinical and social need for the system by surveying the evidence on the scale and consequences of psychological distress among students. Second, it audits the technical landscape, conversational agents, machine-learning methods, sentiment analysis, validated screening instruments, and recommender systems, so that the design choices made later in the report can be defended against, or distinguished from, prior art. A review that only celebrated previous achievements would be of little use; the more valuable contribution is a critical reading that exposes the strengths and weaknesses of existing systems and, in doing so, isolates the precise gap that the present work attempts to fill.

Accordingly, this chapter is organised to move from the problem to the methods and finally to the gap. Sections 2.2 establishes the human problem. Sections 2.3 to 2.7 examine the four methodological pillars on which MindMate rests: conversational agents, machine learning in mental health, sentiment analysis and natural language processing, validated screening instruments, and personalisation through recommender systems and clustering. Section 2.8 draws the threads together in a comparative analysis of representative existing systems, presented in a structured table. Section 2.9 reviews the software-engineering process models that informed how the system was actually built, and Section 2.10 articulates the research gap that motivates the entire project. Throughout, the discussion is deliberately critical: every technique surveyed is examined not only for what it makes possible but also for the limitations and risks it carries, because a mental-health system that ignores those risks would be irresponsible regardless of its technical elegance.

It is worth stating at the outset that MindMate is conceived as a *first-line wellbeing support tool* for students and not as a diagnostic or therapeutic instrument. This framing matters for the review, because much of the literature concerns clinical populations and clinically validated interventions, whereas the present system occupies the lighter-touch, preventive and self-management space. Reading the clinical evidence with that distinction in mind allows the project to borrow what is transferable, validated questionnaires, evidence-based coping strategies, safety practices, without overclaiming a clinical status it does not possess.

## 2.2 Mental Health Among Students

The case for a student-focused mental-health tool begins with the scale of the problem. The World Health Organization's *World mental health report* (World Health Organization, 2022) documents that mental-health conditions remain among the leading contributors to the global burden of disease, that a substantial proportion of these conditions emerge before early adulthood, and that the gap between the number of people who need care and the number who actually receive it remains wide across most of the world. The report is particularly emphatic that early intervention and accessible, community-based support are more effective and more humane than waiting for conditions to escalate to the point of crisis. For a population of students, who are typically in the very age band in which disorders first appear, this argues strongly for low-barrier, preventive support delivered where young people already are, namely on their phones and laptops.

The student population is not merely a smaller version of the general adult population; it carries a distinct and elevated risk profile. The WHO World Mental Health Surveys International College Student Project, reported by Auerbach et al. (2018), gathered data across multiple countries and found that a large fraction of first-year university students screened positive for at least one common mental disorder, with anxiety and mood disorders especially prevalent. Crucially, the same study found that only a minority of affected students sought treatment, a treatment gap that the authors attributed to a combination of stigma, a preference for self-reliance, low perceived need, and practical barriers such as cost and availability. The picture that emerges is of a population that is simultaneously high in need and low in help-seeking, precisely the combination that a discreet, self-directed digital tool is well placed to address.

These international findings are corroborated and enriched by campus-level research. Eisenberg et al. (2013), drawing on survey data from numerous American colleges and universities, documented substantial variation in the prevalence of depression, anxiety, and suicidal ideation both across student subgroups and across institutions. Their work is important for two reasons. First, it confirms that distress is common rather than exceptional within student bodies. Second, by demonstrating that prevalence varies systematically with characteristics such as financial stress, identity, and campus context, it implies that one-size-fits-all support is likely to be suboptimal. A student carrying acute financial pressure and poor sleep is not in the same situation as one experiencing transient examination anxiety, and an effective tool should be able to recognise and respond to that difference. This insight, that heterogeneity within the student population is the norm, directly motivates the personalisation component of MindMate described in later chapters.

Taken together, the literature establishes three facts that frame the entire project. Psychological distress among students is highly prevalent; most affected students do not access formal care; and the needs within the population are heterogeneous. A system that is accessible, non-stigmatising, and adaptive to individual circumstances therefore addresses a genuine and well-evidenced need rather than a hypothetical one. At the same time, the same literature counsels humility: the consequences of distress can be severe, including self-harm and suicide, which means that any tool entering this space must treat user safety as its first responsibility rather than an afterthought.

## 2.3 Conversational Agents in Mental Health

The idea of a computer program that converses about a person's emotional state is far older than the modern smartphone. The lineage begins with ELIZA, the program described by Weizenbaum (1966), which simulated a Rogerian psychotherapist by means of simple pattern-matching and template substitution. ELIZA had no understanding of language in any meaningful sense; it detected keywords and reflected the user's own statements back as questions. Yet Weizenbaum observed, to his own discomfort, that people readily attributed understanding and empathy to the program and were willing to disclose personal feelings to it. This phenomenon, later called the ELIZA effect, is foundational for the whole field, because it demonstrates both the promise and the peril of conversational mental-health software. The promise is that even a simple, transparent, rule-based system can elicit disclosure and provide a sense of being heard. The peril is that users may over-trust a system that does not, and cannot, understand them, which places a heavy ethical burden on designers to avoid deception and to build in safeguards.

The decades since ELIZA have seen conversational agents grow from curiosities into clinically evaluated interventions, and the contemporary evidence base, though still young, is encouraging. Fitzpatrick et al. (2017) conducted a randomised controlled trial of Woebot, a text-based agent that delivers principles of cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) to young adults. Over a two-week period, participants who used Woebot showed significantly greater reductions in symptoms of depression than a control group given an information resource, and engagement with the agent was high. Around the same time, Fulmer et al. (2018) reported a randomised controlled trial of Tess, an integrative agent, and likewise found significant reductions in symptoms of depression and anxiety among college students relative to a control condition. Inkster et al. (2018), studying the empathy-driven agent Wysa with real-world usage data rather than a controlled trial, found that users who engaged more heavily with the agent reported greater improvements in mood, while also cautioning that observational data of this kind cannot establish causation. These three studies are frequently cited together because they represent some of the first credible evidence that automated conversational agents can produce measurable, if modest, benefits in non-clinical and sub-clinical populations.

Broader reviews place these individual results in context and temper any uncritical enthusiasm. Vaidyam et al. (2019) reviewed the psychiatric landscape of chatbots and concluded that, although early findings are promising and patient satisfaction is often high, the evidence base remains small, the trials are typically short, and standardised outcome measurement and long-term follow-up are largely absent. Abd-Alrazaq et al. (2019), in a scoping review of the features of mental-health chatbots, mapped the field and found wide variation in design, in the underlying dialogue technology, the therapeutic framework employed, the platforms used, and, importantly, the extent to which safety and crisis handling were addressed. Provoost et al. (2017), reviewing embodied conversational agents in clinical psychology, similarly observed that while such agents are technically feasible and often well received, rigorous effectiveness evidence is thin and methodological quality is uneven. The consistent message across these reviews is that conversational agents are a promising but immature technology whose benefits are real but bounded, and whose risks, particularly around safety, privacy, and the management of user expectations, are not yet uniformly well handled.

Two limitations recur in this literature and are directly relevant to MindMate. The first is the question of safety: a conversational agent will inevitably encounter users in acute distress, and the reviews note that not all systems handle disclosures of self-harm or suicidal ideation adequately. The second is opacity. Many modern agents are built on large, proprietary language models whose internal reasoning is neither transparent to the user nor inspectable by clinicians or researchers, which complicates trust, accountability, and clinical governance. MindMate responds to both concerns deliberately: it screens every message for crisis risk before generating any reply, and it favours transparent, rule-based and inspectable components, using a generative language model only as an optional enhancement with a fully functional offline fallback. In this sense the project is informed not only by what the conversational-agent literature has achieved but, more importantly, by the gaps and cautions that that literature itself identifies.

## 2.4 Machine Learning in Mental Health

Beyond dialogue, the second technical pillar of the project is machine learning, which has become increasingly central to digital mental health over the past decade. Shatte et al. (2019) conducted a scoping review of machine-learning methods and applications in mental health and identified four broad clusters of use: detection and diagnosis, prognosis and treatment prediction, public-health and surveillance applications, and clinical administration. Their review found that supervised learning dominates the field, that a wide range of data sources is exploited, from questionnaire responses and electronic health records to social-media text and smartphone sensor streams, and that the most common goal is the prediction of a mental-health status or outcome from observable features. This taxonomy is helpful for positioning MindMate, whose stress classifier is a straightforward instance of supervised prediction from a small set of interpretable features, and whose student segmenter is an example of the unsupervised pattern-discovery that Shatte et al. note is comparatively less common but valuable for stratifying populations.

D'Alfonso (2020) offers a more reflective overview of artificial intelligence in mental health, emphasising that the techniques are tools in service of care rather than replacements for human judgement. The paper highlights both the opportunities, scalability, continuous monitoring, the ability to surface patterns that clinicians might miss, and the hazards, including the risk of biased or unrepresentative training data, the danger of over-reliance on automated outputs, and the ethical importance of transparency and informed consent. For a student project this is a salutary reminder that a model achieving high accuracy is not, by itself, evidence of a good system; the way the model's outputs are framed, explained, and acted upon matters at least as much.

Torous et al. (2021) survey the broader field of digital psychiatry, encompassing apps, social media, chatbots, and virtual reality, and provide perhaps the most balanced account of where the field stands. They note genuine progress and a growing evidence base, but they are forthright about persistent problems: many consumer mental-health apps lack any evidence of effectiveness, data-privacy practices are frequently weak, engagement tends to decay rapidly over time, and the gap between research prototypes and clinically governed products remains wide. Their call is for digital tools that are evidence-informed, transparent, privacy-respecting, and designed with the realities of sustained engagement in mind. These criteria read almost as a specification, and MindMate is consciously designed against them: it uses evidence-based instruments and strategies, exposes its reasoning rather than hiding it, stores data locally to respect privacy, and treats safety as foundational. The literature on machine learning in mental health, in short, supplies not only the methods the project uses but also the normative standards against which the project should be judged.

## 2.5 Sentiment Analysis and NLP

A central requirement of MindMate is the ability to read the emotional tone of free text, a chat message or a mood-tracker note, and respond appropriately. This capability falls within sentiment analysis, a subfield of natural language processing concerned with the computational treatment of opinion, affect, and subjectivity in text. Liu (2012) provides the canonical treatment of the area, framing sentiment analysis as the task of identifying and quantifying the polarity (positive, negative, or neutral) and, in richer formulations, the specific emotions expressed in language. Liu distinguishes between document-level, sentence-level, and aspect-level analysis, and surveys the two dominant methodological families: lexicon-based approaches, which score text by reference to dictionaries of affect-bearing words, and machine-learning approaches, which learn to classify sentiment from labelled examples. Liu is careful to enumerate the difficulties that make the task hard in practice, negation, intensification, sarcasm, domain dependence, and the brevity and informality of much user-generated text.

For short, informal, emotionally charged text of the kind a student might type into a wellbeing app, a particularly well-suited tool is VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment Reasoner), introduced by Hutto and Gilbert (2014). VADER is a lexicon-and-rule-based model tuned specifically for social-media language. Beyond a curated lexicon of words rated for valence, it incorporates heuristics that handle several of the very phenomena Liu identifies as challenging: it accounts for punctuation (an exclamation mark intensifies sentiment), capitalisation, degree modifiers such as *very* or *slightly*, contrastive conjunctions such as *but*, and negation. Importantly for this project, VADER requires no training data and produces a transparent, deterministic compound score in the range from minus one to plus one. Hutto and Gilbert reported that VADER's performance on social-media text was competitive with, and in some cases exceeded, that of individual human raters, while remaining computationally trivial and fully interpretable.

The choice of a lexicon-based method over a trained or neural classifier is a deliberate one that the literature supports for this context. A rule-based model is transparent, one can always explain why a given sentence received its score, it runs instantly and offline without any external service, and it does not require a labelled training corpus that a small student project could not realistically assemble. These properties align with the transparency and offline-capability goals that recur throughout the present work. The limitations are equally clear and are acknowledged honestly: a lexicon model cannot capture deep context, can be misled by sarcasm or unusual phrasing, and offers only coarse polarity rather than fine-grained clinical insight. For this reason MindMate treats sentiment as one signal among several and supplements VADER with a small custom emotion-keyword lexicon to detect specific affective states such as anxiety, sadness, or anger, which feed the personalisation logic. Sentiment analysis, in this design, is a lightweight, explainable sensor rather than a clinical judgement.

## 2.6 Screening Instruments (PHQ-9 and GAD-7)

Whereas sentiment analysis offers a soft, indirect reading of emotional state, structured self-report questionnaires provide a standardised, validated, and clinically meaningful measure. MindMate incorporates two of the most widely used brief screening instruments in the world: the Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) for depressive symptoms and the Generalized Anxiety Disorder-7 (GAD-7) for anxiety. The PHQ-9 was validated by Kroenke et al. (2001), who showed that its nine items, each mapping onto a diagnostic criterion for major depressive disorder, form a reliable and valid measure of depression severity. Respondents rate how often they have been bothered by each symptom over the preceding two weeks on a four-point scale from zero (*not at all*) to three (*nearly every day*), yielding a total score from zero to twenty-seven. Kroenke et al. proposed and validated severity bands, minimal, mild, moderate, moderately severe, and severe, that allow a raw score to be interpreted meaningfully. Of particular relevance to a safety-conscious system, the ninth item of the PHQ-9 directly asks about thoughts of self-harm or being better off dead, which is why MindMate treats any non-zero response to that item as a trigger for a self-harm safeguard rather than a mere number contributing to a total.

The GAD-7 was developed and validated by Spitzer et al. (2006) as a brief, parallel measure for generalized anxiety. Its seven items use the same two-week reference period and the same zero-to-three response scale, producing a total from zero to twenty-one, again with published cut-points distinguishing minimal, mild, moderate, and severe anxiety. Spitzer et al. demonstrated good reliability, validity, and the ability of the instrument to discriminate anxiety from other conditions, and the GAD-7 has since become a standard screening tool in both research and routine care. The two instruments are frequently administered together because depression and anxiety commonly co-occur, and using both gives a more rounded picture of a person's psychological state than either alone.

It is worth noting that these are not the only validated instruments available. The Depression Anxiety Stress Scales (DASS), described by Lovibond and Lovibond (1995), offer an alternative that separately measures depression, anxiety, and stress and that was constructed to distinguish these related but distinct negative emotional states. The DASS is a respected instrument and its three-factor structure is conceptually attractive for a project that explicitly models stress. The decision to adopt the PHQ-9 and GAD-7 rather than the DASS rests on several practical considerations: the PHQ-9 and GAD-7 are shorter and quicker to complete, which matters for engagement; they are extremely widely recognised, easing comparison with the chatbot trials reviewed earlier; and the PHQ-9's explicit self-harm item provides a built-in safety hook that aligns directly with the project's safety-first philosophy. Two important caveats apply to the use of any such instrument in MindMate. First, these are *screening* tools, not diagnostic ones; an elevated score indicates that further assessment by a qualified professional may be warranted, not that a disorder is present. Second, self-report scores must always be interpreted with care, since they can be influenced by mood at the moment of completion, social desirability, and honest self-knowledge. MindMate therefore presents screening results as informative indicators framed by appropriate guidance, not as verdicts.

## 2.7 Personalisation and Recommender Systems

The third objective of the project is to personalise support, and the relevant literature here is that of recommender systems together with the clustering and clinical-psychology foundations that underpin a meaningful, individualised response. Ricci et al. (2015), in the standard handbook on the subject, define recommender systems as software that suggests items likely to be of interest or value to a particular user, and they survey the principal families of technique: content-based filtering, which recommends items similar to those a user has liked before; collaborative filtering, which exploits patterns of preference across many users; and knowledge-based and hybrid approaches that combine multiple signals and explicit domain rules. The handbook also stresses two themes that are central to MindMate's design. The first is the importance of context: the *right* recommendation depends not only on who the user is but on their current situation. The second is the value of explanation; a recommendation that the user understands and trusts is more likely to be acted upon than an opaque one, and the literature treats explainability as a feature that improves both effectiveness and user confidence.

In a mental-health context, however, classic collaborative filtering is poorly suited, because the small user base of a student project cannot supply the dense preference data such methods require, and because borrowing one person's coping preferences for another raises ethical and clinical concerns. MindMate therefore adopts a transparent hybrid approach in which coping strategies are scored against several explicit, interpretable signals, the emotion detected in the user's text, the support segment to which the user belongs, and the level of stress predicted by the classifier, with each recommendation accompanied by a human-readable rationale. This design directly reflects the handbook's emphasis on context-awareness and explanation while sidestepping the data and ethical limitations of collaborative methods.

The segmentation that feeds the personalisation rests on clustering, and specifically on the K-Means algorithm. The method originates with MacQueen (1967), who introduced a procedure for partitioning a set of multivariate observations into a fixed number of groups by iteratively assigning points to the nearest cluster centre and recomputing those centres to minimise within-cluster variance. K-Means is unsupervised, requiring no labelled data, which suits the discovery of natural groupings among students based on their wellbeing features; it is also efficient and widely understood, though it requires the number of clusters to be chosen in advance and is sensitive to feature scaling, both of which the implementation addresses. The clinical substance of the recommendations, finally, draws on cognitive therapy as articulated by Beck (1979), whose foundational work established that emotional disorders are maintained by patterns of distorted thinking and that systematically identifying and reframing those patterns can relieve distress. Several of the coping strategies MindMate offers, most explicitly the thought-reframing exercise, but also behavioural activation and structured worry time, are direct descendants of this cognitive-behavioural tradition. Personalisation in MindMate is therefore not merely a matter of statistical matching; it connects an interpretable model of the individual to evidence-based psychological techniques, with the chain of reasoning kept visible throughout.

## 2.8 Comparative Analysis of Existing Systems

Having surveyed the methods individually, it is instructive to compare a representative set of conversational mental-health systems directly, in order to see where MindMate sits relative to prior art. The systems chosen are ELIZA, as the historical baseline; Woebot, Wysa, and Tess, as the most prominent and best-evidenced contemporary agents; and MindMate itself. They are compared along four dimensions that the preceding review identified as critical: the underlying technical approach, the degree of personalisation, the handling of user safety, and the openness or transparency of the system. The comparison is summarised in the table below.

| System | Approach | Personalisation | Safety handling | Open/Transparent |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ELIZA | Pattern-matching and template substitution; no real language understanding | None, identical behaviour for all users | None, no crisis detection or escalation | Conceptually transparent but never designed for real-world support |
| Woebot | Scripted, CBT-based dialogue trees; RCT-evidenced | Limited, adapts within scripted flows | Built-in crisis signposting to external resources | Proprietary; underlying logic not openly inspectable |
| Wysa | Empathy-driven AI agent with CBT and mindfulness content | Moderate, tailors content to expressed mood | Crisis detection with signposting | Proprietary; commercial product |
| Tess | Integrative psychological AI delivering multiple modalities | Moderate, adapts modality to user input | Includes escalation pathways | Proprietary; commercial product |
| MindMate | Hybrid: transparent ML (sentiment, K-Means segmentation, stress classification) plus optional generative model with offline rule-based fallback | High and explainable, emotion, segment, and predicted stress drive scored recommendations with rationale | Rule-based crisis screening before every reply; three risk levels; conservative by design | Fully transparent and open methodology; inspectable components; offline-capable |

Several patterns are visible in this comparison. ELIZA, for all its historical importance, offers no personalisation, no safety handling, and was never intended as a genuine support tool; it is included to mark the distance the field has travelled. The three modern commercial agents, Woebot, Wysa, and Tess, represent a clear advance: each has some empirical evidence behind it, each personalises to a degree, and each incorporates at least basic crisis signposting. Their common limitation, from the standpoint of this project, is opacity. They are proprietary products whose internal logic, data practices, and decision rules are not openly inspectable, which is precisely the concern that Torous et al. (2021) raise about much of the digital-mental-health marketplace. A student, a clinician, or a researcher cannot easily examine why one of these systems said what it said.

MindMate's distinctive position is therefore not that it does something the commercial systems cannot, but that it combines a set of properties that are rarely found together. It treats safety as the first action on every message rather than as an occasional signpost; it makes its personalisation fully explainable, exposing the segment, the predicted stress, the six features, and the recommendation rationale on a dedicated insights page; and it is designed to work entirely offline through a rule-based fallback, so that it never depends on the availability or expense of an external service. None of these properties is individually novel, but their combination in a transparent, open, student-focused tool is uncommon, and it is this combination that the comparative analysis brings into focus and that Section 2.10 frames as the research gap.

## 2.9 Software Development Models

A literature review for an engineering project would be incomplete without considering how the system is built, not merely what it does. Software-engineering scholarship offers several process models, and the two most relevant poles are the plan-driven waterfall model and the family of agile, iterative approaches. Sommerville (2016) describes the waterfall model as a sequential process in which requirements, design, implementation, integration, and maintenance follow one another in fixed phases, with each phase signed off before the next begins. Its strengths are discipline, documentation, and predictability, which make it suitable for large projects with stable, well-understood requirements. Its well-known weakness is rigidity: because real requirements rarely freeze at the outset, the cost of accommodating change late in a waterfall project is high, and problems discovered during implementation can force expensive backtracking.

Agile and iterative models were developed in response to precisely this rigidity. Sommerville (2016) and Pressman and Maxim (2014) both characterise iterative development as a process of building the system in small increments, each delivering working functionality, with frequent opportunities to review, test, and adjust. Rather than attempting to specify everything in advance, an iterative team evolves the requirements and the design together through repeated cycles. The advantages are responsiveness to change, early and continuous validation, and the early visibility of working software; the trade-offs are a lighter documentation burden and a need for discipline to prevent scope drift. Pressman and Maxim emphasise that the choice of process should be governed by the characteristics of the project, its size, the stability of its requirements, the experience of the team, and the consequences of error, rather than by dogma.

MindMate was developed using an iterative, incremental approach rather than a strict waterfall, and the reasons follow directly from these characteristics. The project is the work of a single student exploring a domain in which requirements could not be fully known in advance: it was not clear at the outset exactly how the sentiment signal, the clustering, the stress classifier, and the recommender would best fit together, nor how the safety layer should be tuned, and these questions could only be answered by building and testing successive versions. An iterative approach allowed each component, sentiment analysis, segmentation, classification, the recommender, the safety screen, and the user interface, to be implemented, validated against tests, and refined before the next was layered on. This is reflected in the project's substantial automated test suite, which embodies the continuous-validation ethos that iterative development depends upon, and in the layered architecture that emerged from successive refinement rather than from a single up-front design. At the same time, certain plan-driven practices were retained where they added value, most notably clear documentation of requirements and design and the use of validated, pre-specified instruments and metrics, producing in effect a pragmatic hybrid. This balance, iterative in construction, disciplined in evaluation and documentation, matches the guidance of both Sommerville (2016) and Pressman and Maxim (2014) that the process should fit the project rather than the reverse.

## 2.10 Research Gap

The preceding review supports a clear and specific conclusion about what is missing from the current landscape, and it is this gap that MindMate is designed to address. The need is not in doubt: distress among students is highly prevalent, most affected students do not seek formal help, and the consequences of unaddressed distress can be severe (World Health Organization, 2022; Auerbach et al., 2018; Eisenberg et al., 2013). The methods are largely available: conversational agents can elicit disclosure and deliver evidence-based content (Fitzpatrick et al., 2017; Fulmer et al., 2018), machine learning can stratify populations and predict outcomes from interpretable features (Shatte et al., 2019; D'Alfonso, 2020), lightweight sentiment analysis can read emotional tone transparently (Hutto & Gilbert, 2014), validated questionnaires can quantify symptom severity (Kroenke et al., 2001; Spitzer et al., 2006), and hybrid recommender techniques grounded in cognitive therapy can deliver explainable, individualised support (Ricci et al., 2015; Beck, 1979).

What the review also makes clear, however, is that these strengths rarely come packaged together in a way that is simultaneously transparent, safety-first, offline-capable, and genuinely personalised for students. The reviews of the field repeatedly flag opacity, weak data-privacy practices, inconsistent safety handling, and dependence on external services as recurring shortcomings of existing tools (Vaidyam et al., 2019; Torous et al., 2021; Abd-Alrazaq et al., 2019). The most prominent commercial agents are effective but proprietary and cloud-dependent; the historical and experimental systems are transparent but unsafe or unpersonalised; and very few tools expose their reasoning to the people they serve.

The research gap that MindMate addresses can therefore be stated precisely. It is the absence of a *transparent, safety-first, offline-capable, and personalised first-line wellbeing tool for students*, one that screens every interaction for crisis risk before responding, that explains its personalisation rather than hiding it behind a proprietary model, that functions fully without any external service so that it respects privacy and remains available regardless of connectivity or cost, and that grounds its recommendations in validated instruments and evidence-based psychological techniques. MindMate does not claim to be a clinical instrument, nor to replace professional care, and its evaluation in this report rests on synthetic data that demonstrates a methodology rather than proving an effect on real people. Within those honest limits, the system occupies the under-served space that the literature exposes: accessible, explainable, and safe self-directed support for a population that is large, at risk, and reluctant to seek help through conventional channels. The chapters that follow set out how this gap is translated into a concrete system design, implementation, and evaluation.
