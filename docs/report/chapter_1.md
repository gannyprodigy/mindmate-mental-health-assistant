# Abstract

This report presents **MindMate, An AI-Powered Mental Health Assistant for Students**, a privacy-respecting web application designed to offer accessible, stigma-free, first-line wellbeing support to a student population that faces rising psychological strain and limited access to professional counselling. The objective of the project was to design and develop an AI mental-health assistant, to personalise its support using machine learning, and to evaluate its effect on mental-health and academic outcomes. The system was built in **Python** with a **Streamlit** multipage interface, **scikit-learn** for a K-Means student segmenter and a Logistic-Regression stress classifier, the **VADER** lexicon for sentiment analysis, and a conversational layer that uses the **OpenAI Chat Completions API** when available and a fully **offline rule-based engine** otherwise, with all records held in **SQLite**. A rule-based crisis-safety layer screens every message before any reply is generated. Development followed an **iterative, Agile-influenced methodology** of incremental builds and continuous testing. On synthetic but reproducible data, the segmenter achieved a silhouette score of **0.209** across four meaningful segments, while the stress classifier reached **0.900** accuracy and a **macro-F1 of 0.889**. A simulated four-week pilot comparing treatment and control groups produced large effect sizes, including a Cohen's *d* of **-1.62** for PHQ-9 reduction. These results, obtained on simulated data, demonstrate a sound evaluation methodology rather than clinical efficacy, and position MindMate as a transparent, safety-first complement to existing student support services.

# Chapter 1: Introduction

## 1.1 Background of the Project

Mental ill-health among young people has become one of the defining public-health challenges of the present decade. The World Health Organization estimates that mental disorders affect a substantial share of the global population and that the majority of lifetime conditions begin before early adulthood, yet most affected individuals never receive adequate care (World Health Organization, 2022). University students sit precisely within this high-risk window. The transition into higher education combines academic pressure, financial uncertainty, social upheaval, separation from established support networks, and, frequently, the first experience of living independently. These stressors arrive at the same developmental stage at which many serious conditions first emerge, making the student years a period of unusual vulnerability.

The scale of the problem is well documented. The World Health Organization World Mental Health Surveys International College Student Project found that roughly a third of first-year university students across multiple countries screened positive for at least one common mental disorder, with anxiety and mood disorders especially prevalent (Auerbach et al., 2018). Campus-based research in the United States reaches similar conclusions, reporting high and persistent rates of depression and anxiety that vary across student subgroups and institutions but remain consistently elevated relative to the general population (Eisenberg et al., 2013). These conditions are not merely matters of personal distress; they are strongly associated with reduced concentration, impaired academic performance, lower retention, and, in the most serious cases, self-harm and suicidal ideation. The academic and human costs are therefore deeply intertwined.

Against this rising demand stands a chronic shortage of supply. University counselling services are frequently overstretched, with waiting lists that can extend for weeks during peak periods such as examinations. Even where services exist, they operate within fixed office hours and finite staffing, which means that the moment a student most needs support, late at night, during an anxiety spike, or in the hours before a deadline, is often precisely the moment when no human counsellor is available. Compounding the capacity gap is the persistent stigma surrounding mental health. Many students hesitate to disclose distress to a counsellor, a tutor, or a peer for fear of being judged, labelled, or treated differently, and this reluctance can delay help-seeking until a problem has escalated.

Digital tools offer a pragmatic response to this mismatch between need and capacity. The field of digital psychiatry has matured rapidly, and a growing evidence base indicates that smartphone applications, chatbots, and other digital interventions can extend the reach of mental-health support, lower barriers to entry, and provide continuous, on-demand contact at a scale that human services alone cannot match (Torous et al., 2021). Such tools are well suited to students, a population that is digitally fluent, accustomed to managing aspects of life through applications, and often more willing to engage candidly with a private interface than with a person. Digital support is not a substitute for clinical care, but it can serve as an accessible first point of contact, a means of self-monitoring, and a bridge that helps students recognise when professional help is warranted. It is within this context, high need, constrained capacity, persistent stigma, and a digitally engaged audience, that MindMate is situated.

## 1.2 Problem Statement

The core problem that MindMate addresses is the gap between the volume of student mental-health need and the availability of timely, low-barrier support. Students who experience stress, low mood, or anxiety frequently lack an immediate, private, and non-judgemental avenue through which to express what they are feeling, to make sense of it, and to take a small constructive step. Traditional counselling, while essential, cannot be available to every student at every hour, and the stigma attached to seeking help further suppresses early engagement. The result is that distress often goes unspoken until it becomes severe.

MindMate is designed to provide **accessible, stigma-free, always-available, and personalised first-line support**. It offers a supportive conversational space that a student can use at any time and from any device, without an appointment and without disclosing their identity to another person. Rather than treating every user identically, the system personalises its tone and its recommendations using machine learning, so that the support offered reflects the individual's recent mood, screening scores, sentiment, and engagement patterns. Crucially, the system is built around a safety-first principle: every message is screened for indications of crisis before any reply is produced, and where serious risk is detected the application steps back from conversation and directs the student to qualified human help and verified helplines.

It is equally important to state clearly what the problem is *not*. MindMate is **not** a clinical instrument and does not attempt to replace professional diagnosis or treatment. It does not deliver therapy, prescribe interventions, or manage acute emergencies. The problem it solves is the absence of an accessible, supportive, and intelligently personalised first layer of care, one that can listen, signpost, encourage healthy coping, and, when necessary, escalate to human services. Positioning the tool honestly in this way is itself part of solving the problem responsibly.

## 1.3 Objectives

The project is organised around three objectives drawn directly from the project brief.

**Objective 1: Design and develop an AI mental-health assistant for students.** The first aim is to build a complete, working application that students can actually use, rather than a research prototype confined to a notebook. This entails designing an intuitive multipage interface, implementing a supportive conversational engine, providing structured features for mood tracking and validated self-screening, and ensuring that the whole system operates reliably, including a guarantee that it continues to function even without an external language-model service. The assistant must be approachable, private, and safe by design.

**Objective 2: Personalise its support using machine-learning algorithms.** The second aim is to move beyond a one-size-fits-all assistant by embedding machine learning at the heart of the experience. This involves segmenting students into meaningful support groups using unsupervised clustering, predicting stress level using a supervised classifier, analysing the sentiment of free-text input, and feeding these signals into a transparent recommender that selects appropriate coping strategies. Personalisation is intended to be explainable: a student should be able to see why a given recommendation was made.

**Objective 3: Evaluate its effectiveness on mental-health and academic outcomes.** The third aim is to assess, in a methodologically rigorous manner, whether the assistant plausibly improves outcomes. Because real-participant trials lie beyond the scope of an academic project, evaluation is conducted on carefully constructed synthetic data that encodes realistic relationships. The evaluation measures changes in standardised mental-health indicators and in academic focus, applies appropriate statistical tests, and reports effect sizes, thereby demonstrating a complete and credible evaluation pipeline.

## 1.4 Scope of the Project

The scope of MindMate is defined deliberately so that the system remains both useful and responsible.

**In scope.** The project delivers the following capabilities:

- **Conversational wellbeing support** through an empathetic chat interface that adapts its tone to the user's state.
- **Mood tracking**, allowing students to log daily mood, energy, and sleep, with sentiment analysis of free-text notes and trend visualisation.
- **Validated self-screening** using the PHQ-9 and GAD-7 questionnaires with their published scoring and severity bands (Kroenke et al., 2001; Spitzer et al., 2006).
- **Machine-learning personalisation**, including student segmentation, stress prediction, and an explainable coping-strategy recommender.
- **A transparent insights view** that exposes the features and reasoning behind the system's outputs.
- **A curated resources library** of psychoeducational material, coping strategies, and verified crisis helplines covering both Indian and global services.

**Out of scope.** The project explicitly excludes:

- **Clinical diagnosis** of any mental-health condition. Screening tools indicate severity for self-awareness and signposting only.
- **Treatment or therapy.** MindMate does not deliver a structured clinical intervention and is not a medical device.
- **Emergency response.** The system cannot dispatch help or guarantee a human response; in crisis situations it signposts to qualified services rather than attempting to manage the situation itself.

This boundary is not a limitation imposed reluctantly but a core design decision. A first-line support tool that overstates its competence would be ethically unacceptable, so the scope is drawn to keep the system helpful, honest, and safe.

## 1.5 Existing System Overview

Several conversational mental-health applications already exist and have informed the design of MindMate. The earliest demonstration of conversational interaction with a computer, ELIZA, showed decades ago that people will engage emotionally with a text-based agent even when its responses are generated by simple pattern matching (Weizenbaum, 1966). Contemporary systems are considerably more sophisticated.

**Woebot** is a fully automated conversational agent that delivers principles of cognitive behavioural therapy through scripted dialogue. In a randomised controlled trial with young adults, it was associated with a significant reduction in symptoms of depression over a two-week period, providing some of the strongest early evidence that a chatbot can produce measurable benefit (Fitzpatrick et al., 2017). **Wysa** is an empathy-driven agent that combines conversational support with self-help tools; a real-world, mixed-methods evaluation found that users who engaged more heavily with the application reported greater improvements in mood, suggesting a dose-response relationship between engagement and benefit (Inkster et al., 2018). **Tess** is a psychological AI agent designed to deliver on-demand emotional support; a randomised controlled trial reported reductions in self-identified symptoms of depression and anxiety among students who interacted with it (Fulmer et al., 2018).

Alongside these products sits conventional, human-delivered university counselling, which remains the gold standard for depth of care but is constrained by capacity, cost, opening hours, and the stigma that deters some students from attending. Reviews of the wider landscape note that while conversational agents show promise, many are closed, commercial systems whose internal logic is opaque to the user, and that few are tailored to the specific pressures of student life or designed with transparent, inspectable personalisation. These observations directly motivate the design choices behind the proposed system.

## 1.6 Proposed System Overview

MindMate is proposed as a supportive conversational wellbeing assistant for students that combines the accessibility of existing chatbots with three differentiating commitments: transparent machine-learning personalisation, a safety-first architecture, and guaranteed offline capability.

The system is delivered as a multipage Streamlit web application. A student can converse with the assistant, log and review their mood over time, complete validated PHQ-9 and GAD-7 self-checks, inspect the reasoning behind the system's outputs, and access a library of coping strategies and helplines. Beneath this interface, six canonical features, average mood, average sleep, PHQ-9 score, GAD-7 score, average sentiment, and engagement, are computed for each user and passed to the machine-learning layer. A K-Means segmenter assigns the student to one of four meaningful support groups, and a Logistic-Regression classifier predicts their stress level. These outputs, together with the emotion detected in the student's own words, drive a hybrid recommender that selects coping strategies and, importantly, returns a plain-language rationale for each suggestion.

The first differentiator is **transparency**. Where many existing applications present personalisation as a black box, MindMate exposes the features it uses, the segment it has inferred, the stress probabilities it has computed, and the reasoning behind each recommendation, so that the student can understand and challenge the system's view of them. The second is **safety-first design**: a rule-based crisis-detection layer screens every message before any reply is generated, classifying risk as none, elevated, or crisis, and responding conservatively so that a false alarm is always preferred to a missed signal. In a crisis case the system declines to counsel and instead provides a validating message and verified helplines. The third is **offline capability**: although the assistant can use a hosted language model when one is configured, it ships with a complete rule-based fallback engine, ensuring that the application remains fully functional, private, and free to run without any external dependency. Together these properties distinguish MindMate from comparable tools and align it with the responsible-use principles emphasised in the digital-psychiatry literature (Torous et al., 2021).

## 1.7 Technologies Used

The implementation draws on a focused, open stack of well-established technologies, each chosen for a specific role.

- **Python 3.12** is the implementation language, providing a mature ecosystem for data science and rapid application development.
- **Streamlit** provides the user interface, with multipage navigation, enabling a complete interactive web application to be built directly in Python.
- **scikit-learn** supplies the machine-learning components, K-Means clustering, Logistic Regression, standardisation, pipelines, and PCA, and is the foundation of the personalisation layer (Pedregosa et al., 2011).
- **VADER**, together with a custom emotion-keyword lexicon, performs lexicon-based sentiment analysis on free-text input, which is robust on short, informal messages (Hutto & Gilbert, 2014).
- The **OpenAI Chat Completions API** powers the conversational replies when a key is available, while a fully **offline rule-based fallback engine** guarantees that the assistant always works without one.
- **pandas, NumPy, and SciPy** handle data manipulation and statistical analysis, and **plotly** and **matplotlib** provide in-app and report visualisations respectively.
- **SQLite**, accessed through Python's `sqlite3` module, provides lightweight local persistence, while **joblib** serialises trained models and **python-dotenv** manages configuration.
- **pytest** underpins the testing strategy, with a suite of automated tests covering safety, sentiment, screening, persistence, and the machine-learning components.
