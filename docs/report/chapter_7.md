# Chapter 7: Results and Discussion

## 7.1 Introduction

This chapter presents and interprets the empirical results produced by the MindMate system. It brings together the outputs of the three machine-learning components that personalise the assistant, the K-Means student segmenter, the multinomial Logistic-Regression stress classifier, and the VADER-based sentiment analyser, together with the results of a simulated pilot evaluation designed to assess the system's effect on mental-health and academic outcomes. Each result is first reported using the exact figures generated by the reproducible analysis pipeline and is then discussed in terms of what it reveals about the underlying data and the behaviour of the models.

Before presenting any numbers, one caveat must be stated plainly because it frames the interpretation of everything that follows. **All of the data analysed in this chapter are synthetic.** The 600-student feature dataset and the 240-participant pilot study were both generated programmatically from latent-variable models that encode realistic but artificial relationships between wellbeing variables. Consequently, the results reported here demonstrate that the evaluation *methodology* is sound, reproducible, and capable of detecting the effects it is designed to detect; they do **not** constitute evidence of real-world clinical efficacy. This distinction is restated at several points throughout the chapter, and most forcefully in the Limitations section, so that no reader mistakes a methodological demonstration for a clinical claim. The reproducibility of the synthetic generation (random seed 42) means that every statistic below can be regenerated exactly, which is itself a virtue: it makes the analysis transparent and auditable, even though it does not make it clinical.

## 7.2 Dataset Overview

The primary modelling dataset, `data/synthetic_students.csv`, contains 600 simulated students, each described by the six canonical wellbeing features used throughout MindMate: average mood (`avg_mood`, 1-5), average sleep duration (`avg_sleep_hours`), the PHQ-9 depression score (`phq9_score`, 0-27), the GAD-7 anxiety score (`gad7_score`, 0-21), average journal sentiment (`avg_sentiment`, -1 to +1) and an engagement count (`engagement`). Each record additionally carries a categorical `stress_label` of Low, Moderate or High that serves as the target for the supervised classifier. The records were produced by a latent-wellbeing generator that first samples an unobserved wellbeing state for each student and then derives the six observable features from it, deliberately injecting the kinds of correlation that clinical literature would lead us to expect, for example, that poorer sleep co-occurs with higher anxiety, and that lower mood co-occurs with elevated PHQ-9 and GAD-7 scores.

The presence of these designed-in correlations is confirmed by the correlation matrix of the six features.

[[FIG: fig_correlation.png | Figure 7.1: Correlation matrix of the six wellbeing features]]

As shown in Figure 7.1, the standardised features exhibit a coherent block structure. The two clinical screening instruments, `phq9_score` and `gad7_score`, are strongly positively correlated with one another, which is consistent with the well-documented comorbidity of depressive and anxiety symptoms (Kroenke et al., 2001; Spitzer et al., 2006). Both of these scores are negatively correlated with `avg_mood`, `avg_sleep_hours` and `avg_sentiment`: students whose generated state places them under greater symptom load also tend to report lower mood, shorter sleep and more negative journal sentiment. Engagement shows weaker associations with the remaining variables, behaving as a relatively independent dimension that captures how much a student interacts with the application rather than how distressed they are. This correlation structure is important for two reasons. First, it validates that the synthetic generator behaves as intended rather than producing noise. Second, it explains in advance why both unsupervised and supervised models are able to extract a usable signal: the features are neither perfectly collinear (which would make some redundant) nor independent (which would leave nothing to learn), but instead occupy the productive middle ground where structure exists to be discovered.

The second dataset, `data/evaluation_study.csv`, supports the pilot analysis in Section 7.6. It simulates a four-week study of 240 participants, split evenly into 120 treatment users who notionally used MindMate and 120 control users who did not, with pre- and post-intervention measurements of PHQ-9, GAD-7, a wellbeing index (0-100) and weekly focus hours.

## 7.3 Student Segmentation Results

The first personalisation component is an unsupervised K-Means segmenter (MacQueen, 1967; Lloyd, 1982) that groups students into four support segments on the basis of their standardised six-feature profiles. The number of clusters, k = 4, was chosen to map onto four interpretable levels of support need rather than being derived purely from an internal optimisation criterion. To ensure that the resulting labels are meaningful rather than arbitrary, each cluster centroid was scored on a composite wellbeing index and the four clusters were then ranked and named accordingly: **Thriving**, **Coping**, **At-Risk** and **High-Need**.

The four segments are unequal in size, as Figure 7.2 illustrates.

[[FIG: fig_segment_distribution.png | Figure 7.2: Distribution of students across the four support segments]]

The segment sizes are At-Risk with 198 students, Coping with 176, Thriving with 119 and High-Need with 107. The two middle-and-lower segments (At-Risk and Coping) together account for 374 of the 600 students, leaving a smaller Thriving group and a still smaller but clinically most important High-Need group of 107. This distribution is plausible for a student population: a large mass of individuals carry moderate concerns, a minority are flourishing, and a comparable minority require the most active support. From a product perspective, the relatively small High-Need cluster is precisely the group toward which the assistant should escalate its recommendations and resource signposting most assertively.

The internal quality of the clustering was assessed using the silhouette coefficient (Rousseeuw, 1987), which yielded a value of **0.209**. The silhouette coefficient ranges from -1 to +1, where values near 1 indicate tight, well-separated clusters, values near 0 indicate overlapping clusters whose boundaries are fuzzy, and negative values indicate misassignment. A score of 0.209 is modest and honestly reflects the nature of the data: wellbeing is a continuum, not a set of discrete natural kinds, so any partition of students into segments will inevitably cut through regions of gradual transition rather than through empty gaps. The PCA projection in Figure 7.3 makes this visible.

[[FIG: fig_segment_pca.png | Figure 7.3: PCA projection of the student segments]]

When the six-dimensional feature space is reduced to its first two principal components, the four segments appear ordered along a dominant axis that runs from High-Need at one extreme to Thriving at the other, with Coping and At-Risk occupying the intermediate territory. The clusters are clearly arranged but their edges touch and partly overlap, which is exactly what a silhouette score around 0.2 predicts. Rather than treating this as a defect, it is more accurate to read it as a faithful representation of the construct: a student near the boundary between At-Risk and Coping genuinely is a borderline case, and forcing such a student into one bucket is a modelling convenience, not a claim that two truly distinct populations exist. For MindMate's purposes the segmentation does not need crisp separation to be useful; it needs only to provide a stable, interpretable coarse stratification that biases the tone and recommendation mix of the assistant in a sensible direction, which it achieves.

## 7.4 Stress Classification Results

Whereas segmentation is unsupervised and operates at the level of broad support tiers, the stress classifier is a supervised model that predicts a discrete stress level, Low, Moderate or High, for an individual student from the same six features. The model is a multinomial Logistic Regression implemented with scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al., 2011) inside a pipeline that first standardises the features. The model was evaluated on a stratified 25% hold-out test set comprising 150 samples, so that the reported metrics reflect performance on data the model never saw during training.

On this hold-out set the classifier achieved an **accuracy of 0.900** and a **macro-averaged F1 score of 0.889**. The closeness of these two figures is itself informative: macro-F1 averages the per-class F1 scores with equal weight regardless of class frequency, so a macro-F1 nearly as high as the accuracy indicates that the model performs well across all three classes rather than achieving a high overall score by neglecting a minority class. The per-class breakdown confirms this.

| Class | Precision | Recall | F1-score | Support |
|-------|-----------|--------|----------|---------|
| High | 0.932 | 0.958 | 0.945 | 72 |
| Low | 0.941 | 0.865 | 0.901 | 37 |
| Moderate | 0.810 | 0.829 | 0.819 | 41 |
| **Macro avg** | **0.894** | **0.884** | **0.889** | **150** |

The strongest performance is on the High class, with an F1 of 0.945 and a recall of 0.958. This is the most operationally desirable error profile, because the High-stress category is the one MindMate most needs to detect reliably; a recall of 0.958 means that very few genuinely high-stress students are missed. The Low class also performs well (F1 0.901), with high precision (0.941) but somewhat lower recall (0.865), indicating that a few low-stress students are pulled into the adjacent Moderate band, a conservative direction of error that errs toward offering more support rather than less. The weakest class is Moderate (F1 0.819), which is unsurprising and easily explained: Moderate is the middle category that shares borders with both Low and High, so the feature profiles that fall into it are the least distinctive and the most likely to be confused with their neighbours. The confusion matrix makes this pattern explicit.

[[FIG: fig_confusion_matrix.png | Figure 7.4: Confusion matrix of the stress classifier]]

As Figure 7.4 shows, the errors are overwhelmingly confined to adjacent classes, Low confused with Moderate, and Moderate confused with High, rather than the catastrophic Low-versus-High confusions that would be most damaging. There are essentially no instances of a high-stress student being labelled low-stress or vice versa. This ordinal coherence is the natural consequence of using a linear model on features that are themselves monotonically related to the underlying stress construct: a Logistic Regression learns a set of separating hyperplanes, and when the true classes are ordered along a continuum, the misclassifications it makes are concentrated at the boundaries between neighbouring bands. For a wellbeing assistant, this is the right failure mode, because a one-step error (treating a Moderate student as High, or vice versa) carries little practical cost, whereas a two-step error could either alarm a stable student or overlook a struggling one.

## 7.5 Sentiment Analysis Results

The third analytical component is sentiment analysis, applied to the free-text notes students write in the Mood Tracker. MindMate uses VADER (Hutto & Gilbert, 2014), a lexicon- and rule-based model well suited to short, informal, first-person text, augmented with a custom emotion-keyword lexicon. VADER returns a compound polarity score in the range -1 to +1, which is stored alongside each note and feeds the `avg_sentiment` feature consumed by the segmenter and classifier.

Because sentiment is computed per note and accumulates over time, its most informative output is longitudinal rather than cross-sectional. Figure 7.5 traces the sentiment of one simulated student's journal notes across successive entries.

[[FIG: fig_sentiment_trend.png | Figure 7.5: Sentiment of a student's journal notes over time]]

The trajectory in Figure 7.5 shows the characteristic volatility of day-to-day affect overlaid on a slower underlying trend: individual entries swing between positive and negative polarity, but the smoothed signal reveals a gradual movement that would otherwise be invisible in any single note. This is precisely the value that automated sentiment tracking adds. A student is poorly placed to judge their own mood trend from memory, whereas a continuously computed sentiment series can surface a slow drift toward more negative language before it crystallises into a clinically significant change. Within MindMate the aggregated sentiment also serves a second, structural role: by feeding `avg_sentiment` into the personalisation models, it allows the textual channel (what a student writes) to influence the same segment and stress predictions that are otherwise driven by numeric inputs (mood ratings, sleep, screening scores), so that the assistant responds to expressed emotion as well as reported numbers.

The principal limitation of this component is intrinsic to lexicon-based methods: VADER reasons from word polarity and a small set of grammatical rules, so it can be misled by sarcasm, irony, negation across long spans, and domain-specific idiom. The custom emotion lexicon mitigates some of this for the student-wellbeing domain, but the approach remains a deliberately lightweight, transparent and privacy-preserving choice rather than a state-of-the-art neural one. For the purposes of this project that trade-off is appropriate, because interpretability and on-device operation without sending text to an external service were weighted above marginal gains in accuracy.

## 7.6 Pilot Evaluation of Effectiveness

The third project objective is to evaluate the assistant's effectiveness on mental-health and academic outcomes. This was addressed through a simulated four-week pilot study with 240 participants, 120 assigned to a treatment condition (notional MindMate users) and 120 to a control condition. Four outcomes were measured before and after the study period: PHQ-9 (depression), GAD-7 (anxiety), a wellbeing index scaled 0-100, and weekly focus hours as an academic-engagement proxy. The analysis uses change scores (post minus pre) and compares the two groups with Welch's t-test, which does not assume equal variances between groups. Effect sizes are reported as Cohen's d and interpreted using the conventional thresholds (Cohen, 1988), under which d of roughly 0.2, 0.5 and 0.8 denote small, medium and large effects respectively.

It bears repeating before the numbers are read that **this pilot is conducted on synthetic participants**. The data were generated to embody a plausible treatment effect, so the analysis below demonstrates that the evaluation design and statistics can recover and quantify such an effect; it is not, and must not be read as, evidence that MindMate produces these changes in real students.

The pre/post comparison of the two groups is summarised in Figure 7.6, and the formal test statistics are given in the table that follows.

[[FIG: fig_evaluation_prepost.png | Figure 7.6: Pre/post comparison of treatment vs control]]

| Metric | Treatment Δ | Control Δ | t | p | Cohen's d |
|--------|------------|-----------|-------|---------|-----------|
| PHQ-9 | -3.90 | -0.87 | -12.581 | < 0.001 | -1.624 |
| GAD-7 | -3.39 | -0.90 | -11.413 | < 0.001 | -1.473 |
| Wellbeing | +11.35 | +2.52 | 14.312 | < 0.001 | 1.848 |
| Focus hours | +4.26 | +0.69 | 11.151 | < 0.001 | 1.440 |

Across all four outcomes the treatment group changed substantially more than the control group, and in every case the difference is statistically significant at p < 0.001. For the two clinical measures, the treatment group's PHQ-9 fell by 3.90 points against the control's 0.87, and its GAD-7 fell by 3.39 points against 0.90; the underlying group means move from 13.35 to 9.45 (treatment PHQ-9) versus 13.85 to 12.99 (control), and from 9.93 to 6.54 (treatment GAD-7) versus 10.40 to 9.49 (control). For the wellbeing index the treatment group rose by 11.35 points (46.54 to 57.90) against the control's 2.52 (44.96 to 47.48), and focus hours rose by 4.26 (14.30 to 18.56) against 0.69 (13.93 to 14.62). A paired t-test on the treatment group's wellbeing pre versus post returns t = 24.271, p < 0.001, confirming that the within-group improvement is itself reliable and not an artefact of between-group variance.

The effect sizes, shown in Figure 7.7, are the headline result and the one that most needs careful interpretation.

[[FIG: fig_effect_sizes.png | Figure 7.7: Effect sizes by outcome measure]]

Every Cohen's d in the table exceeds 1.4 in magnitude, PHQ-9 at -1.624, GAD-7 at -1.473, wellbeing at 1.848 and focus hours at 1.440. Under Cohen's (1988) conventions all four are well beyond the 0.8 threshold for a large effect; they would, in a real study, be regarded as very large. Two caveats temper this immediately. First, and most importantly, these magnitudes are a property of the synthetic data-generating process: the simulation was parameterised to contain a clear treatment signal, so recovering a large d demonstrates the sensitivity of the analysis rather than the potency of the intervention. Second, even in genuine field studies, effect sizes of this magnitude are rarely observed for digital mental-health tools, and a real deployment of MindMate would be expected to yield considerably smaller effects subject to attrition, placebo response, and the gap between controlled and naturalistic use. The honest reading of Section 7.6, therefore, is that MindMate is equipped with a rigorous, reproducible evaluation harness capable of detecting and quantifying intervention effects with appropriate statistics, and that this harness behaves correctly on data of known structure.

## 7.7 Discussion

Taken together, the four sets of results tell a coherent story about why the models behave as they do. The common thread is the correlation structure established in Section 7.2. Because the synthetic generator encodes genuine, monotonic relationships between the wellbeing features, every downstream model has a real signal to exploit, and the *form* of each model's success and failure follows directly from that structure.

The segmenter's modest silhouette of 0.209 and the classifier's high accuracy of 0.900 are not in tension; they are two views of the same underlying continuum. Unsupervised clustering struggles to find crisp boundaries precisely because wellbeing varies smoothly, which depresses the silhouette score, yet supervised classification thrives because that same smooth variation makes the classes linearly separable along an ordinal axis once labels are supplied. In other words, the data are hard to *partition* but easy to *order*, and each model is judged on the task it is given. The classifier's concentration of errors in the Moderate class and at adjacent-class boundaries (Section 7.4) is the supervised echo of the cluster overlap seen in the PCA projection (Section 7.3): both phenomena arise because the middle of a continuum is intrinsically the least distinctive region.

The sentiment component connects the textual and numeric channels and, through the `avg_sentiment` feature, ensures that what a student writes can influence the same personalisation pathways as what they record numerically. This integration is a deliberate design choice that increases the robustness of the personalisation: a student who downplays their mood ratings but writes increasingly negative notes will still be nudged toward greater support. The pilot evaluation then closes the loop by demonstrating that the project possesses a credible methodology for the third objective, measuring outcomes, even though the demonstration is run on simulated participants.

A further point of discussion concerns the explainability of the whole pipeline. None of the personalisation models is a black box: the segments are named by ranking centroids on an interpretable wellbeing index, the Logistic Regression exposes per-class probabilities and coefficient-driven reasoning, and the recommender returns an explicit rationale string for every suggestion. This transparency is a considered response to the well-known reluctance of clinicians and users to trust opaque automated recommendations in a sensitive domain (Torous et al., 2021; D'Alfonso, 2020), and it is one of the system's more defensible strengths irrespective of the synthetic nature of the evaluation.

## 7.8 Comparison with Existing Systems

It is useful to situate these results against the established conversational mental-health agents reviewed in earlier chapters. Woebot, evaluated in a randomized controlled trial, reported significant reductions in depressive symptoms among young adults over a two-week period using a fully automated cognitive-behavioural conversational agent (Fitzpatrick et al., 2017). Wysa, evaluated on real-world usage data, found that users with higher engagement showed greater improvement in self-reported mood (Inkster et al., 2018). Tess similarly demonstrated reductions in depression and anxiety symptoms in a randomized trial (Fulmer et al., 2018).

MindMate is broadly aligned with these systems in its conception, an empathetic, always-available conversational agent that draws on cognitive-behavioural principles (Beck, 1979) and incorporates a crisis-safety layer, but it differs in two respects that the present results highlight. First, MindMate foregrounds *transparent machine-learning personalisation*: where the comparator systems are primarily scripted or dialogue-driven, MindMate explicitly segments users, classifies stress, and explains its recommendations, surfacing the analytical reasoning of Sections 7.3 to 7.5 directly to the user through its Insights page. Second, and crucially, the comparison must be qualified by the nature of the evidence. The Woebot, Wysa and Tess findings rest on data from *real human participants*, whereas the pilot in Section 7.6 rests on *synthetic participants*. The large effect sizes reported here cannot therefore be placed on the same evidential footing as those studies; they show that MindMate's evaluation design is methodologically comparable to the published trials, not that its outcomes are. A fair summary is that MindMate matches the architecture and ambition of these systems and exceeds some of them in explainability, while remaining, at this stage, an unvalidated prototype awaiting the real-world evaluation that the comparator systems have already undergone.

## 7.9 Limitations

The results in this chapter must be read subject to a number of limitations, the most significant of which has already been stated repeatedly but warrants a final, unambiguous restatement.

- **Synthetic data throughout.** Both the 600-student modelling dataset and the 240-participant pilot are simulated. Every metric, the silhouette of 0.209, the accuracy of 0.900, the macro-F1 of 0.889 and especially the large Cohen's d values exceeding 1.4, describes the system's behaviour on artificial data generated with a known structure. These results validate the analytical and evaluation methodology; they are not evidence of clinical efficacy in real students, and the very large effect sizes in particular should be expected to shrink substantially in any genuine deployment.

- **Modest cluster separation.** The silhouette score of 0.209 indicates overlapping segments. The segmentation is useful as a coarse, interpretable stratification but should not be presented to users as a sharp categorical diagnosis.

- **Limited classifier scope.** The stress classifier is a linear model over six engineered features and predicts a three-level ordinal label. Its weakest class is the Moderate middle band, and its strong performance is partly a function of the clean, correlated synthetic features; performance on noisier real data would likely be lower.

- **Sentiment analysis constraints.** The VADER-based analyser cannot reliably handle sarcasm, irony, complex negation or domain-specific idiom, and was chosen for transparency and privacy rather than maximal accuracy.

- **Pilot design constraints.** The simulated pilot runs over only four weeks, models no attrition, placebo response or non-adherence, and assumes a clean treatment signal. A real study would require ethics approval, informed consent, longer follow-up, and would contend with all of these complications.

- **Not a clinical instrument.** MindMate is a supportive wellbeing tool with a conservative rule-based crisis layer, not a diagnostic or therapeutic device. The PHQ-9 and GAD-7 screenings it administers are validated questionnaires (Kroenke et al., 2001; Spitzer et al., 2006) used for self-awareness and signposting, not for diagnosis.

In summary, this chapter has demonstrated that MindMate's machine-learning components extract a coherent and interpretable signal from the wellbeing features, that its stress classifier performs strongly and fails gracefully, and that its evaluation harness can detect and quantify intervention effects with appropriate statistics. These are genuine and valuable outcomes for a project of this scope. They establish that the methodology is correct and reproducible, while leaving the question of real-world effectiveness explicitly open for future work on human participants.
